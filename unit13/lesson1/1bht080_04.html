Infectivity

Infectivity refers to how easily the pathogen infects a host. For example, the chicken pox and measles viruses are very “infective,” easily infecting a susceptible host. On the other hand, the bacteria causing tuberculosis is not very infective.

Some diseases, such as botulism, are non-contagious, meaning they are transmitted only from the source to a susceptible person.  Other diseases, such as smallpox or influenza, are contagious and may spread rapidly from one person to another through populations and across geographic regions.  A rapid spread of a contagious disease could have a significant impact on an installation’s mission.
